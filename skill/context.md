## CUSTOMER ENGAGEMENT MODEL (CEM) — SALESFORCE STAGES

**Deal drop-off stats (last 497 records): 35% at S1 | 25% at S2 | 18% at S3 | <5% after S3**
→ If you nail S0–S3, you're almost on coast mode. 78% of losses happen in S1–S3.

**The 3 Whys (required at every stage transition)**
- Why Amplitude? (vs. alternatives)
- Why Change? (what's broken with status quo)
- Why Now? (urgency — why not next quarter)

| Stage | Name | Exit Criteria | Key Owner |
|-------|------|--------------|-----------|
| S0 | SDR Engagement | ICP confirmed, S1 booked, AE intro done | SDR |
| S1 | Discovery | Discovery call done, pain/urgency/3 Whys documented, Champion/EB identified, FLM validation | AE |
| S2 | Qualification | Full MEDDPICC validated, MAP initiated ($100K+ deals), Value Map initiated, FLM sign-off | AE + SE |
| S3 | Business & Technical Evaluation | Eval Plan agreed, Technical Win Plan in place, BVA delivered, EB engagement, MAP updated | SE accountable |
| S4 | Negotiation | — | AE |

**MEDDPICC**: Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Paper Process, Identify Pain, Champion, Competition.

**FLM Validation**: Front Line Manager must validate the 3 Whys before any opp moves to S2. Quality gate for real, winnable deals.

**MAP (Mutual Action Plan)**: Mutually agreed plan with ownership, due dates, tasks across both orgs. AE owns it. Organic — evolves with the deal.

**Value Map**: Sweet spot S2+. Too early at S0/S1. Too late at S3/S4 when decisions already made.

**POC vs. POV**:
- POC (Proof of Concept): Will it physically work? Architecture, integration, data migration. Technical buyer audience.
- POV (Proof of Value): When it works, does it deliver the value promised? Aligns to Value Map. Executive buyer audience.

## CHALLENGER SALES METHODOLOGY

**Why it matters**: Buyers are 57% through their purchase decision before first seller contact (likely 80–88% in AI era). 53% of buyer loyalty comes from the SALES EXPERIENCE — not brand, product, or price. It's YOU.

### Five Seller Profiles (% of top performers)
| Profile | % Top Performers | Strength | Weakness |
|---------|-----------------|----------|----------|
| Relationship Builder | 4% | Trust | People-pleasing, won't push |
| Hard Worker | 10% | Volume | Grinds but no insight |
| Problem Solver | 7% | Reliable | Reactive, not proactive |
| Lone Wolf | 25% | Self-driven | Alienating, solo |
| **Challenger** | **54%** | Insight + urgency | — |

**You don't have to be 100% Challenger all the time. These are levers. Find micro-moments to insert Challenger behaviors.**

### The Three T's: Teach, Tailor, Take Control

**Teach (for Differentiation)**
- Deliver commercial insight in a rational AND emotionally compelling way
- Lead TO the solution, don't lead WITH it
- A good teacher: motivating, credible, comfortable with confrontation, transfers knowledge

**Tailor (for Resonance)**
- Customize message to individual priorities using the 4 research layers
- Make them feel seen, heard, designed specifically for them
- Golden Rule: Every interaction should feel like it was built for them — because it was

**Take Control**
- Guide the customer through the buying process
- Create tension purposefully
- Take Control ≠ dominating; it means guiding, not dictating
- Constructive tension maps to Take Control

**How they connect**: Research enables Teaching → Teaching enables Tailoring → Both enable Taking Control = faster deal velocity, more money.

## CONSTRUCTIVE TENSION FRAMEWORK

The tug of war metaphor: productive push and pull IS the conversation. If you drop the rope, there's nothing.
Key question: **Is the pain of staying the same greater than the pain of change?**

### Three Tension Zones

**No Tension (Passive)**
- Customer leaning back, one-word answers, no urgency to act
- Fix: Use research-based, ego-centric open-ended questions
- Best question: "What do you care about?" → brain lights up, they talk about themselves
- Also: "What have you heard about Amplitude?" (vs. closed "Have you heard of us?")

**Constructive Tension (Assertive) ← SWEET SPOT**
- Customer leaning forward, intrigued, asking questions, may seem skeptical (skepticism = gears turning = green flag)
- There's a need for action. Productive, moving toward change.
- Definition: A productive force that compels a customer to change
- WE create it. If we don't call or email, there's no tension. We control the dial.

**Too Much Tension (Aggressive)**
- Customer stressed, arms crossed, defensive, refusing to act
- Often triggered by blockers
- Fix: "Turn anger into passion" — "It feels like this is something you're really passionate about. What's driving that?"
- Acknowledge, don't fight. Breathe through the silence.

### Video Reference Examples (from cAMP session)
- Wolf of Wall Street ("Sell me this pen") → Passive to Aggressive — silence as a lever
- Tommy Boy (brake pads) → Too much tension — aggression shuts down engagement
- The Office (Dunder Mifflin vs. big guys) → Constructive — research-based questions, acknowledged reality
- Papa Sal's Pizza (The Internship) → Masterclass in all 3 T's: acknowledged fear, taught with data, tailored to artisanal approach, took control

## RESEARCH FOUNDATION (4 Layers)

**"Research is formalized curiosity. It is poking and prying with a purpose." — Zora Neale Hurston**

### Layer 1: Industry (Macro credibility)
- Biggest positive/negative change right now?
- Top 3 news stories making waves?
- How are peers responding?

### Layer 2: Company (Specificity = your angle)
- How do they make money? (Revenue model)
- What's their value proposition?
- Senior leadership's top priorities this year?
- Know their products, app names, loyalty programs
- Read earnings calls, investor reports

### Layer 3: Role (Their functional world)
- What do they own and deliver?
- How are they measured this quarter?
- Budget authority? Org position?
- Target roles: Product, Marketing, Growth, Data & Analytics, Engineering, UX, Finance

### Layer 4: Individual (The Differentiator — where YOU add value AI can't)
- Personal/professional goals — promotion, recognition, innovation champion?
- Communication style — data-driven or casual? Email or Slack?
- Attitude toward Amplitude — warm, skeptical, neutral?
- People buy from people they like. An ego stroke based on real research opens doors.

**JT's Warning on AI**: Nobody reads 16 pages of AI output. Learn to "gist" — get the TLDR, extract what's useful, add your soul. AI is fallible. It's a starting point. The individual layer is where YOU differentiate.

## THREE BUYER PERSONAS

### MOBILIZERS — Your Target (The Teachers)
- Champions of change who drive internal consensus, sell on your behalf inside the org
- Signals: Ask tough, probing questions (skepticism = GREEN FLAG), use "we" language, idea-loyal not vendor-loyal, exist at ALL levels
- Activity clues: Asking implementation questions, bringing in new stakeholders (legal, IT, finance), building a business case, procurement getting involved
- Key insight: A blocker who flips to a mobilizer becomes your most powerful internal seller
- Invest your time here

### TALKERS — Be Careful (The Friend)
- Friendly, approachable, willing to talk. BUT lack internal influence to drive decisions.
- Risks: Info may flow to competitors. Creates illusion of momentum. Time on talkers = time NOT on mobilizers.
- Pro tip: Use talkers for background intelligence (org dynamics, key players, sentiment). Never rely on them to move the deal.
- Test: After the call, was there a real CTA? Did they follow through? If not, talker.
- The conference swag collector who won't stay at your booth = classic talker

### BLOCKERS — Route Around Them (The Enemy)
- Resistant to change, fiercely protective of status quo, actively work against your deal
- Why they block: Past vendor trauma, fear job is threatened, ego (you're "calling their baby ugly"), "if it ain't broke..."
- Strategy: Don't argue — reframe. Build broader coalition with mobilizers. Avoid direct confrontation.

## COMMERCIAL TEACHING STRUCTURE (The Choreographed Conversation)

**Old way**: Company overview → ROI discussion → "Buy from us" → Leading WITH strengths
**Challenger way**: Their business concerns → Cost of status quo → Your unique solution → Leading TO strengths

### Phase 1: Share the Reframe (Why Change?)
Build credibility by reframing their perception of a relevant business issue.
- **Current State**: Empathetically describe their status quo. "Today, you have..." / "Customers like you tell us they spend a lot of time on..."
- **Transition ("However...")**: "The reality is..." / "Surprisingly..." / "What you're not considering is..."
- **Reveal the Gap**: Show the flaw in current thinking. "Similar companies continue to overlook..." / "This is actually slowing you down..."

*If your reframe is wrong: Don't panic. Pivot. Ask an open-ended question. Sometimes being slightly wrong on purpose gets them to open up.*

### Phase 2: Introduce Impact (Why Now?) — The Valley of Despair
Intensify rational consequences. Humanize the problem. Make them feel the cost of staying the same.
- Know their numbers: how are they losing revenue, increasing risk, increasing expenses?
- Use opportunity cost, benchmarks, case studies of companies that didn't change
- Make it emotional — the goal is to get the buyer to the point where they're "crying" about unsolvable challenges
- Storytelling tools: Imagery (invoke 5 senses), Analogies ("like shoveling snow with a rake"), Vivid Equivalents (connect abstract data to concrete measures)

### Phase 3: Reveal Value (Why Us?)
Demonstrate the value of adopting a new way. Show the way out of the valley.
- Customer stories from similar companies/industries
- ROI examples and business cases
- How Amplitude uniquely solves the problem they now feel viscerally

### Turning Monologue into Dialogue (Questions at Each Phase)
- During Reframe: "How often do you see this issue?" / "How do you currently handle it?"
- During Impact: "What concerns you about these percentages?" / "What would you do if you found out this was happening?"
- During Value: "How do you see this being implemented?" / "What would you address first with limited resources?"

## VALUE MAPPING FRAMEWORK

A living, breathing document capturing core business context, use cases, KPIs, and how Amplitude delivers value. Used presale AND post-sale (EBRs).

### The Four Columns
| Column | Content |
|--------|---------|
| Value Driver | Acquisition, Retention, Monetization, or Operational Efficiency |
| Business Objectives | What specific goals/challenges? |
| Measures of Success | Specific numbers, metrics, dates, percentages |
| How Amplitude Helps | Features, workflows, use cases tied to their goals |

### When to Value Map
- S2+ (qualification and beyond). S0/S1 usually too early.
- Done WITH the customer, not AT them. Show your screen. Let them correct you. It's collaborative.
- Can be done live (most impactful) or async (for APAC or shorter call cultures)
- Organic — evolves through presale → sale → post-sale → renewal → EBR
- Not every account: Strat accounts = always. Velocity accounts = pick your top ones.

### Marsha/PGA Tour Video Lessons
- "Help me understand" repeatedly — open-ended, active listening
- Show screen and research upfront, let customer knock off what's wrong
- Coming with POV and being OK being wrong — immediately de-highlight and move on
- Critique from room: Could frame crawl/walk/run timeline upfront for phased value delivery

## DEMO BEST PRACTICES (Becca, Emerging Enterprise SE Lead)

**What makes a good demo**:
- Good storytelling + preparation
- Tailored to client's tech stack and industry
- Show don't tell — align to challenges, not feature walkthrough
- Value selling — speak to business goals, not features
- Engaging — ask open-ended questions; don't do all the talking
- Pre-set trap/leading questions — have 5 questions you don't want to forget
- Customer proof points — 5 customer stories in your back pocket (aligned to product area)
- Time management — leave room for next steps and questions
- Big pauses — let silence work (constructive tension in demos too)
- Preparedness — clean screen, tabs pre-loaded, fast mode on Global Agent
- Use demo as discovery — "How does this land?" while showing

**Insert customer stories naturally**: "Oh, by the way, I'm showing you journeys — this is exactly what Instacart uses to understand first-time buyers." Conversational > formal case study slide.

### Three Demo Types
| Type | Duration | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| Discovery demo | <10 min | Harbor tour, quick show-and-tell, get next meeting |
| Platform demo | 30-60 min | Tailored, persona-based, full platform story |
| Deep dive demo | 60-90 min | Specific feature, custom mock data, often on-site |

**"Imagine a world in which..." is no longer acceptable. Custom demos are easy with AI tools now.**
